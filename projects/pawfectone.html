PAWFECT ONE

Awarded Top 5 Finalist out of 250 participants for my first hackathon, nwPlus' HackCamp 2023, a 12-hour hackathon. Pawfect One is an innovative dating app for dog owners, designed to connect dog owners in their quest for companionship â€“ both for themselves and their furry friends. It is based off the stereotype of how dog owners tend to look and act like their dogs. Users swipe through profiles, to find a compatible dog that matches with their personality. Upon a match, the dog's profile is revealed, along with its owner's information.

As a Project Manager and UX/UI Designer for Pawfect One, I was responsible for the website UX/UI design, wireframing, prototype, visual graphics, mood board, and video editing. All work seen on this page was completed in the span of 12 hours.

THE MAIN CHALLENGE

Our team was predominantly composed of first-time hackers with little to no knowledge on web development or design. We were a rag-tag team of four strangers, and unlike premade teams, did not have a preplanned idea concept. We were tasked with developing a working app within 12 hours, making it difficult to learn while balancing build time.

LEARN DAY

Learn Day was the first phase of the hackathon. It was a day dedicated to learning new skills from presentations and workshops. Attendance was optional, but I came as early as I could to have adequate time to plan out our design. The day was mostly dedicated to getting myself acquainted with my three teammates, discussing our idea and design scope, as well as addressing possible problems that we might face.

MOODBOARD

We decided to first create a moodboard to serve as inspiration for our app. I downloaded a bunch of different dating apps, taking screenshots and recording my interactions with the UI for animations and logic. From there, I compiled them into a master moodboard, making sure to gather input and feedback from my teammates. Additionally, I researched possible colour palettes which aligned with our team's design goals, which was to make an adorable 'kawaii' aesthetic app. However, we would end up doing a website, but this still was useful in terms of content placement.
View the entire moodboard below:
Due to the nature of the hackathon, we weren't allowed to create anything else before Build Day, so our moodboard was the only reference material we had going in to the competition.

BUILD DAY

Build Day was the 12-hour window where we could bring our project to life. During this period, I created the wireframes, Figma prototype, taught my teammate Figma, added the animations and transitions, created the graphics in Illustrator, and editted the presentation video with Premier Pro.

CHECKLIST AND DOCUMENTATION

Ivan and I created a checklist from what we discussed on Learn Day. Featured in the checklist is necessary features for our prototype. I made sure to include these when making my wireframes. We set a time buffer of 2 hours before the deadline to finish, so that we wouldn't be rushed in submitting the project.

WIREFRAMES

The next step was making wireframes for key screens. Since we only had 12 hours to create the entire design, I opted for the fewest key screens so we could maximize their quality. Also on the sheet were iterations of the logo prototypes and graphic layouts which I would have to create in Illustrator.

GRAPHICS AND ASSETS

Next, I opted to make custom graphic assets for the logo, landing, and page UI using a mix of Adobe Illustrator and Photoshop. I wasn't sure about usage rights for our project, so I decided to play it safe by making everything myself.

FIGMA PROTOTYPE

Now came the building of the real website UI. I based the screen designs off of the wireframes, changing and improving some features on the fly. Together, my teammate and I collaborated in creating the screens and adapting the designs in Figma.
The landing and login screens.
The match UI, with the right image displaying a successful match.
Additional features of the app: a map displaying nearby parks, and a settings tab to filter matches.
Due to extra time, I spent two hours hooking up the Figma prototype with animations, transitions, and additional screens for interactivity.
As a final touch, I checked the accessability of the website by applying a greyscale filter over it, making sure it followed the WCAG Web Accessibility guidelines.

PRESENTATION VIDEO

After we were finished our Figma prototype, we began recording the presentation video. I provided a live demonstration of the app, narrating each interaction. I editted the timing of the narration and clips to match, removing stutters and adjusting volume as necessary.

FEEDBACK

At the end of the day, we nervously submitted our project to be judged. It was nerve-wracking as we all waited, anticipating the results. Our project would be judged by our fellow competitiors through peer feedback.

CONCLUSION

To our surprise, we won a Finalist prize! Nothing can describe the elation I felt in the moment, seeing our project's name displayed on the big screen. I felt as though our persistent efforts truly had been worth it. Most of all, I was very happy that our determination to make a fully interactive Figma prototype served proof that hard work does pay off.

In short, I learned that it is best when you take the time to teach others. Secondly, that I should always express my highest efforts for everything in life, and that collaboration is key to success.
